What the Heck, FSF?!

May 11th, 2011

A few nights ago, I recived the following email.

 

Did you know that, like you, nearly 50% of the 40,000 subscribers to the FSF’s supporter mailing list have an @gmail.com email address?

JavaScript, once lauded for adding simple visual effects to web pages, is now used by web sites like Gmail to run powerful

programs on your computer. These programs, like any other program running on your computer, should be free software. But

right now, the vast majority of JavaScript programs do not respect your essential freedoms to run, study, modify and share

them. They take control of your computer away from you; the same control we have been working for over 25 years to protect.

Because of the incredibly high percentage of Gmail-using free software supporters, we’re taking action to raise awareness

about how to use Gmail without using proprietary software. We’ll also be working on longer term solutions, but the most

immediate positive step to take is to stop running the proprietary programs.

Many people suggest that you shouldn’t use Gmail at all, because it means losing control over your data and privacy. We agree

that this is a very important factor for you to consider when choosing how you will handle your email.

 

But since you are already using Gmail, please help us build momentum for change by not using the proprietary JavaScript on

your system – and helping others to do the same.

 

Yours sincerely,

 

Matt Lee

Campaigns Manager

Free Software Foundation

–

Sent from the Free Software Foundation,

51 Franklin Street

Fifth Floor

Boston, MA 02110-1335

United States

 

To this epistle, I have but one thing to say.

 

WAKE UP. NAOW.

Seriously. 

 

I read that, and I tweeted the link. 

Shortly thereafter, I got the following tweet.

firefly911 @jrgifford  That’s pretty odd. I fully support open software, but if someone wants their code proprietary they have that right to.

(Link)

 

So… what’s he saying there? I couldn’t understand it from all the proprietary kool-aid I’ve been drinking.

 

Exactly that. True freedom (I prefer the term “liberty” but that’s another story) is the right to choose. And that means that if they want to, they can make their codebase proprietary. As a matter of fact, I’m writing this on a Ubuntu Linux box (Free software), using the amazing Simplenote (not free) for my editor. Oh, and I’m using Google Chrome (Not completely free, although it does have a large amount of free components in it), because Firefox (Free) is a memory hog on my computer. And (Ya know, before I forget), I’m also using Dropbox (Not free) to backup the stuff I’m writing in Simplenote, and I’ve also got about 5-10 tabs open with * GASP * proprietary websites in them! WHO WOULDA THUNK IT?!?!?!?! O_O

My point is, people are going to use what WORKS. And until there are FOSS (Where FOSS = Free Open Source Softare) alternatives that work as well (or (hopefully) better than) the competition, people won’t be changing. 

 

I know a guy who still uses his AOL account for all his email. 

Why? 

Because it works. And yes, there are “better” alternatives out there. But it works. 

 

And (as was stated in a open letter to RMS) 

“We live in a world where having the technological edge makes the difference between success and failure; asking us to just give up that edge for a theoretical idea of freedom is not going to work.”

And on that note, to the FSF, I have this to say.

 

If you’re really about Freedom, then people have the right to make their codebase (software, artwork, etc) proprietary. And now, if you’ll excuse me, I’ll just go off and play one of those DRM’ed games that I bought for $.99 on the (proprietary) Apple AppStore, because for games, the (“Open Source”-but-not-really) Android just doesn’t seem to work well. Wonder why…
